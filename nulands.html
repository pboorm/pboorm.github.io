Abstract

Hard X-ray-selected samples of Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN) are biased against objects obscured by Compton-thick gas columns, typically found in the circumnuclear environment < 100 pc (nicknamed the pc-scale "torus").

Yet, these sources are vital in understanding the evolution of supermassive black holes across cosmic time. This is the webpage for the NuSTAR Local AGN N(H) Distribution Survey (NuLANDS) - a legacy survey of AGN selected with the InfraRed Astronomy Satellite (IRAS) to have warm infrared colors between 25-60 micron, where AGN emission is expected to be approximately isotropically radiated even for sources buried under extreme columns. We show that optically classified Type 1 and 2 AGN in NuLANDS are indeed indistinguishable in terms of optical and infrared AGN bolometric indicators, while Type 2 AGN are deficient in terms of their observed hard X-ray flux, as expected for obscuration under heavy line-of-sight gas columns. In this sense, NuLANDS provides a more representative sampling of the local Seyfert population than hard X-ray flux-limited selection, and we find a directly observed Compton-thick AGN fraction > 2 times higher than seen with the Swift/Burst Alert Telescope without bias corrections, based upon X-ray spectroscopic characterisation.
